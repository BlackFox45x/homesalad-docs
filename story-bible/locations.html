Locations

This section details the five districts of Greenville and the key locations within each district that form the physical setting of HomeSalad.

Greenville Overview

Greenville is a mid-sized town located in a temperate region with distinct seasons. Once a thriving agricultural and manufacturing center, the town has experienced economic decline but retains a rich cultural heritage and natural resources. The town is divided into five distinct districts, each with its own character, challenges, and opportunities.

Geographic Features

  • River: The Green River runs through the center of town, dividing it roughly in half
  • Hills: Rolling hills surround the town to the north and east
  • Lake: Brightwater Lake lies to the southwest of town
  • Forests: Patches of forest remain, especially in the northern areas
  • Agricultural Land: Farmland surrounds the town, much of it now owned by MegaGrow

The Five Districts

Greenville is divided into five distinct districts, each with its own character, challenges, and opportunities for transformation:

Rust Belt District

Current State: A formerly industrial area now characterized by abandoned factories, warehouses, and worker housing. The district suffers from pollution, unemployment, and deteriorating infrastructure.

Transformation Potential: The Rust Belt District has abundant space for large-scale projects, existing infrastructure that can be repurposed, and a skilled workforce with manufacturing experience. It represents the greatest challenge but also the most dramatic potential for transformation.

Unique Resources: Salvageable industrial materials, large indoor spaces, existing water and power connections, worker knowledge of manufacturing processes.

Key Locations

  • The Workshop: A former machine shop converted into a community makerspace by Jackson Reed and the Rust Belt Reclamation Crew. Serves as a hub for building and crafting activities.
  • Abandoned Factory: A massive former manufacturing facility that can be transformed into an indoor agricultural center, community space, or green technology hub depending on player choices.
  • Worker Housing: Modest homes built for factory workers, many now in disrepair but with good bones and small yards suitable for gardens.
  • Contaminated Creek: A small waterway heavily polluted by industrial runoff, representing a major environmental restoration challenge.
  • Railway Yard: An abandoned rail hub that could be repurposed for transportation or transformed into a community gathering space.

Downtown Commons

Current State: The historic center of Greenville, featuring a mix of civic buildings, local businesses, and public spaces. While some storefronts are vacant, the area retains its traditional character and serves as the community hub.

Transformation Potential: Downtown Commons has high visibility, existing foot traffic, and cultural significance. Projects here have the greatest immediate impact on community morale and can showcase sustainable practices to the widest audience.

Unique Resources: Public spaces, historic architecture, central location, existing community gathering spots, political access.

Key Locations

  • The Greenhouse: A converted storefront that serves as the headquarters for the Green Spark Collective. Features demonstration gardens, a seed library, and meeting spaces.
  • City Hall: The center of local government where council meetings are held and official business is conducted. A key location for political storylines.
  • Farmers Market Plaza: An open square that hosts the weekly farmers market and can be developed into a permanent local food hub.
  • Community Center: A public building with meeting rooms, educational facilities, and space for events. Can be upgraded with sustainable features.
  • MegaGrow Local Office: The corporate presence in town, housed in a modern building that contrasts with the historic architecture around it.

Riverside Heights

Current State: An older residential neighborhood along the Green River, home to many long-time residents and elders. Features traditional homes with established gardens, though the riverbank has suffered from erosion and pollution.

Transformation Potential: Riverside Heights has existing gardening traditions, knowledgeable residents, and a strong sense of community. The river provides opportunities for restoration projects and water management demonstrations.

Unique Resources: Traditional gardening knowledge, established plant varieties, river access, community memory, intergenerational connections.

Key Locations

  • Maria's Garden: The extensive home garden of Maria Gonzalez, featuring heirloom varieties and traditional techniques. Serves as an educational site and seed source.
  • Riverside Park: A public green space along the river that has fallen into disrepair but has potential for restoration and community use.
  • Community Kitchen: A shared cooking and food preservation facility where traditional recipes and techniques are practiced and taught.
  • Eroded Riverbank: A section of the river suffering from erosion and pollution, presenting an opportunity for riparian restoration projects.
  • Heritage Center: A small museum documenting Greenville's history and cultural traditions, which can be expanded with living history demonstrations.

Meadowbrook Suburbs

Current State: A post-war suburban development with single-family homes on modest lots. Characterized by manicured lawns, conventional landscaping, and a mix of working families and professionals.

Transformation Potential: Meadowbrook Suburbs has abundant lawn space that can be converted to productive use, engaged homeowners with resources to invest, and opportunities to demonstrate suburban sustainability.

Unique Resources: Open yard space, engaged families, existing neighborhood associations, educational opportunities, middle-class resources.

Key Locations

  • Neighborhood School: An elementary school with grounds that can be transformed into educational gardens and outdoor classrooms.
  • Model Lawn Conversion: A demonstration home where a conventional lawn has been transformed into an edible landscape, serving as an example for neighbors.
  • Community Pool: A neighborhood swimming facility that can be retrofitted with sustainable water management systems.
  • Neighborhood Park: A conventional park with potential for food forests, native plantings, and natural play areas.
  • HOA Clubhouse: The meeting place for the Homeowners Association, representing both potential resistance to change and a forum for community organizing.

Brightwater Shores

Current State: An area along Brightwater Lake that includes both upscale homes and recreational facilities. While economically advantaged, the district faces water quality issues and tensions between development and conservation.

Transformation Potential: Brightwater Shores has financial resources, influential residents, and direct connections to natural water systems. Projects here can address water quality issues and demonstrate how affluence can be aligned with sustainability.

Unique Resources: Lake access, financial capital, political influence, recreational facilities, scientific expertise among residents.

Key Locations

  • Lakeside Laboratory: A water quality monitoring station that can be expanded into a citizen science center for environmental research.
  • Brightwater Club: An exclusive social club with extensive grounds that could be retrofitted with sustainable features or opened for community use.
  • Wetland Preserve: A protected natural area that has been degraded but could be restored to improve water filtration and wildlife habitat.
  • Luxury Garden: An ornamental garden at a wealthy estate that could be transformed into a showcase for aesthetic and productive sustainable landscaping.
  • Proposed Development Site: A contested area where MegaGrow plans to build corporate facilities, representing a key battleground in the struggle for Greenville's future.

Location Transformation

A core gameplay element in HomeSalad is the visual and functional transformation of locations over time. Each location can progress through several states of transformation:

Transformation Stage Visual Indicators Functional Changes
Degraded Pollution, neglect, sparse vegetation, gray/brown color palette Limited resources, negative environmental effects, restricted access
Initial Improvement Clean-up, basic plantings, simple structures, hints of green Basic resource production, improved access, simple community uses
Established Project Diverse plantings, functional infrastructure, balanced green/built elements Regular resource yields, community gathering, educational opportunities
Thriving System Lush vegetation, integrated design, wildlife presence, vibrant colors Abundant resources, self-maintenance, multiple functions, community pride
Regenerative Showcase Ecosystem integration, artistic elements, seasonal beauty, landmark status Surplus production, innovation hub, tourist attraction, inspiration source

Location-Specific Challenges

Each district presents unique environmental and social challenges that players must address:

Rust Belt District

  • Soil Contamination: Industrial pollutants requiring bioremediation or contained growing systems
  • Infrastructure Decay: Deteriorating buildings and utilities needing repair or creative adaptation
  • Economic Depression: High unemployment and limited resources requiring job creation and skill development
  • Social Stigma: Negative perceptions of the area requiring community pride building and rebranding

Downtown Commons

  • Space Limitations: Dense urban environment requiring vertical and intensive growing methods
  • Competing Uses: Multiple stakeholders with different visions for limited public space
  • Historic Preservation: Balancing sustainability retrofits with preservation of historic character
  • Political Visibility: High-profile projects subject to scrutiny and regulatory hurdles

Riverside Heights

  • Flooding Risk: Riverside properties vulnerable to seasonal flooding requiring water management
  • Aging Population: Elderly residents needing accessible design and intergenerational connections
  • Traditional Mindsets: Resistance to new methods requiring respect for existing knowledge
  • Riparian Degradation: Eroded riverbanks and water pollution requiring restoration techniques

Meadowbrook Suburbs

  • HOA Restrictions: Regulations limiting landscape changes requiring negotiation and demonstration
  • Aesthetic Concerns: Emphasis on conventional beauty requiring design that balances function and appearance
  • Chemical Dependencies: Lawns maintained with fertilizers and pesticides requiring transition strategies
  • Busy Lifestyles: Time-limited residents requiring low-maintenance and convenient solutions

Brightwater Shores

  • Water Quality Issues: Lake pollution from runoff requiring filtration systems and watershed management
  • Exclusive Access: Private property limiting community involvement requiring negotiation and partnerships
  • Luxury Expectations: High standards for aesthetics requiring high-end sustainable design
  • Development Pressure: Valuable land targeted for corporate development requiring conservation strategies

Connecting Locations

While each district has its own character, HomeSalad emphasizes the connections between locations and the flow of resources, knowledge, and people throughout Greenville:

Physical Connections

  • Green River: Connects Riverside Heights, Downtown Commons, and the Rust Belt District, serving as both a challenge (pollution) and opportunity (restoration, transportation)
  • Greenway Trail: A developing path system that can connect all districts, facilitating movement and creating a green corridor
  • Public Transportation: Bus routes that can be expanded or modified to better connect communities and reduce car dependence
  • Watershed: The shared water system that connects all districts, making water quality a common concern

Resource Flows

  • Food Distribution: Systems for moving locally grown food from production areas to food deserts and markets
  • Material Reuse: Salvaged materials from the Rust Belt repurposed for projects in other districts
  • Knowledge Sharing: Traditional practices from Riverside Heights applied in modern contexts elsewhere
  • Financial Investment: Resources from Brightwater Shores supporting projects in less affluent areas

Design Note: The interconnection of locations is a key theme in HomeSalad, emphasizing that true sustainability requires thinking in systems rather than isolated projects. Players are encouraged to develop projects that create beneficial connections between different parts of Greenville.
