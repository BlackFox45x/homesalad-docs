World Design

This section details the world design of HomeSalad, including the layout of Greenville, environmental transformation systems, level design principles, and world interaction mechanics.

World Design Philosophy

HomeSalad's world design is guided by several core principles:

  • Responsive Environment: The world visibly changes in response to player actions, providing clear feedback and a sense of impact
  • Ecological Authenticity: Environmental systems follow realistic ecological principles while remaining accessible and engaging
  • Meaningful Spaces: Each area serves narrative, gameplay, and aesthetic purposes rather than existing solely for exploration
  • Community Context: The world reflects the community's history, values, and social dynamics through its design
  • Layered Discovery: The environment reveals deeper levels of detail and interaction as players develop skills and relationships

World Structure

The world of HomeSalad is centered around the town of Greenville and its surrounding areas, divided into distinct districts with unique characteristics and challenges.

World Scale

HomeSalad uses a compressed scale that balances realism with gameplay considerations:

  • The town of Greenville is approximately 1 square kilometer in gameplay terms
  • Walking from one end of town to the other takes approximately 5-7 minutes of real time
  • Each district is sized to feel distinct while allowing reasonable travel times
  • Scale compression is more pronounced for agricultural and natural areas

World Map

The world map is divided into five main districts, each with distinct visual identity, gameplay opportunities, and environmental challenges:

Rust Belt District (Northwest)

Visual Identity: Industrial decay, abandoned factories, worker housing, rusty infrastructure, sparse vegetation

Gameplay Focus: Material salvaging, building repurposing, soil remediation, industrial-scale projects

Environmental Challenges: Soil contamination, structural decay, water pollution, lack of biodiversity

Downtown Commons (Center)

Visual Identity: Historic buildings, central plaza, mixed-use structures, public spaces, small businesses

Gameplay Focus: Community organizing, public demonstrations, market activities, social hub functions

Environmental Challenges: Limited green space, impervious surfaces, heat island effect, resource distribution

Riverside Heights (Northeast)

Visual Identity: Older homes, established gardens, riverside paths, heritage trees, traditional architecture

Gameplay Focus: Traditional gardening, knowledge preservation, riparian restoration, community traditions

Environmental Challenges: Riverbank erosion, flooding risk, invasive species, aging infrastructure

Meadowbrook Suburbs (Southwest)

Visual Identity: Post-war homes, manicured lawns, cul-de-sacs, schools, parks, uniform aesthetics

Gameplay Focus: Lawn conversion, neighborhood organizing, educational programs, family engagement

Environmental Challenges: Monoculture lawns, chemical dependencies, water waste, social conformity

Brightwater Shores (Southeast)

Visual Identity: Lakeside properties, upscale homes, recreational facilities, scenic viewpoints, decorative landscaping

Gameplay Focus: Water quality improvement, sustainable luxury, influential stakeholder engagement

Environmental Challenges: Shoreline development, runoff pollution, resource-intensive lifestyles, exclusive access

Connectivity

Districts are connected through several systems that facilitate movement and create a cohesive world:

  • Road Network: Main streets and side roads that provide primary navigation routes
  • Green River: A waterway that flows through multiple districts, serving as both boundary and connector
  • Greenway Trails: Pedestrian and bicycle paths that develop as players restore natural corridors
  • Public Transportation: Bus routes that can be improved through community projects
  • Viewsheds: Scenic overlooks that provide visual connections between areas

Environmental Transformation System

A core feature of HomeSalad's world design is the Environmental Transformation System (ETS), which allows players to visibly change the environment through their actions.

Transformation Stages

Most areas in the game can progress through five stages of environmental health:

Stage Visual Characteristics Functional Properties Gameplay Effects
Degraded Pollution, sparse vegetation, gray/brown palette, visible damage Poor soil, contaminated water, minimal biodiversity, resource scarcity Limited production, higher resource costs, negative health effects
Recovering Initial cleanup, pioneer plants, improved maintenance, hints of green Basic soil function, reduced contamination, early succession species Basic resource production, improved work efficiency, neutral health
Functional Established vegetation, clean appearance, balanced built/natural elements Healthy soil, clean water, moderate biodiversity, resource availability Regular resource yields, normal work efficiency, slight health bonus
Thriving Diverse vegetation, integrated systems, wildlife presence, vibrant colors Rich soil, purified water, high biodiversity, abundant resources Abundant yields, improved work efficiency, moderate health bonus
Regenerative Complex ecosystems, self-sustaining systems, peak aesthetic beauty Living soil web, pristine water, maximum biodiversity, surplus resources Maximum yields, optimal efficiency, significant health bonus

Transformation Mechanics

Players transform the environment through several interrelated mechanics:

  • Direct Actions: Planting, building, cleaning, and restoring specific elements
  • System Implementation: Creating water management, soil building, and habitat systems
  • Maintenance Activities: Regular care that prevents degradation and supports improvement
  • Community Projects: Larger-scale transformations that require collective effort
  • Policy Changes: Working with the City Council to implement supportive regulations

Transformation occurs at multiple scales:

  • Micro: Individual garden beds, rain gardens, or habitat features (immediate feedback)
  • Meso: Neighborhood parks, community gardens, or stream sections (medium-term projects)
  • Macro: District-wide green infrastructure, watershed restoration (long-term initiatives)

Transformation Metrics

The game tracks several metrics to determine environmental state:

  • Biodiversity Index: Variety and abundance of plant and animal species
  • Water Quality Rating: Cleanliness and health of water bodies
  • Soil Health Score: Organic matter, microbial activity, and contamination levels
  • Carbon Sequestration: Amount of carbon stored in plants and soil
  • Aesthetic Quality: Visual appeal and sensory experience of the environment

Level Design Principles

While HomeSalad is not a traditional level-based game, its spaces are designed according to specific principles to create engaging gameplay environments.

Spatial Composition

  • Landmarks: Distinctive features that aid navigation and create memorable locations
  • Pathways: Clear routes that guide movement while allowing exploration choices
  • Nodes: Activity centers where multiple systems and interactions converge
  • Edges: Boundaries that define spaces and create transition opportunities
  • Districts: Areas with cohesive identity and consistent design language

Gameplay Spaces

Different types of spaces support specific gameplay activities:

Space Type Primary Function Design Characteristics Examples
Production Spaces Resource generation and cultivation Organized layout, resource access, tool storage, efficient workflows Gardens, workshops, processing areas
Social Spaces Community interaction and organization Gathering areas, seating, visibility, acoustic properties Plazas, community centers, markets
Learning Spaces Knowledge acquisition and sharing Demonstration areas, information displays, practice zones Workshops, libraries, experimental gardens
Restoration Spaces Environmental healing and transformation Before/after contrast, staged progression, visible metrics Contaminated sites, degraded streams, barren lots
Sanctuary Spaces Reflection, rest, and aesthetic enjoyment Sensory richness, privacy, natural elements, comfort Gardens, parks, viewpoints, meditation spots

Environmental Storytelling

The world communicates narrative through environmental elements:

  • Historical Layers: Visible remnants of Greenville's past that reveal its history
  • Character Imprints: Spaces that reflect the personalities and values of their inhabitants
  • Ecological Narratives: Natural processes and relationships made visible through design
  • Social Dynamics: Spatial arrangements that reveal community relationships and power structures
  • Transformation Records: Before/after contrasts that show the impact of player actions

World Interaction Systems

Players interact with the world through several integrated systems:

Navigation System

  • World Map: Zoomable map showing districts, landmarks, and points of interest
  • Wayfinding: Environmental cues, signage, and landmarks that guide exploration
  • Fast Travel: Bicycle and public transportation options that unlock as players progress
  • Pathfinding: Dynamic route suggestions based on current objectives and environmental conditions

Interaction System

  • Contextual Actions: Available interactions based on location, tools, skills, and environmental state
  • Tool Use: Specialized equipment for different environmental interactions
  • Resource Collection: Gathering materials from appropriate sources throughout the world
  • Object Placement: Positioning buildings, plants, and infrastructure in the environment
  • System Management: Controlling water, energy, and material flows in created systems

Feedback System

  • Visual Changes: Immediate and long-term visual transformations that reflect player actions
  • Environmental Metrics: Measurable indicators of environmental health and function
  • NPC Reactions: Character responses to environmental changes and improvements
  • Gameplay Effects: Functional benefits from improved environmental conditions
  • Achievement Markers: Monuments, plaques, and other recognition of significant transformations

World Building Tools

HomeSalad provides players with several tools for shaping the world:

Building System

Players can construct various structures and infrastructure:

  • Buildings: Functional structures like greenhouses, workshops, and community spaces
  • Infrastructure: Water management, energy systems, and transportation elements
  • Furniture: Benches, tables, storage, and other functional objects
  • Decorative Elements: Aesthetic improvements like signs, art, and lighting

Planting System

Players can introduce and manage vegetation:

  • Food Plants: Vegetables, fruits, herbs, and other edible species
  • Functional Plants: Species that provide specific ecosystem services
  • Native Habitat: Indigenous plant communities that support wildlife
  • Ornamental Plants: Decorative species that enhance aesthetic appeal

Terraforming System

Limited ability to modify the terrain:

  • Soil Modification: Adding amendments, mulch, and other soil improvements
  • Water Features: Creating ponds, swales, and other water management elements
  • Landform Adjustments: Minor changes to create berms, basins, and terraces
  • Surface Treatments: Changing ground covers, paths, and paving

Design Note: World design in HomeSalad emphasizes the connection between environmental health and community wellbeing. The transformation of physical spaces serves as both a gameplay mechanic and a metaphor for social transformation, with visible changes in the environment reflecting the community's journey toward sustainability and resilience.
